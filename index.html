TOPIC 1

Lines and Angles

What You Need to Know

An angle is the opening/space between two rays meeting at a point (vertex).

🌍 Curiosity: The ancient Babylonians divided a circle into 360 degrees around 3000 BC! They chose 360 because it's divisible by many numbers (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, etc.), making calculations easier. This is why we still use 360° today!

Key Angle Types

Types of Angles:
  • Acute: 0° - 90° (small, sharp)
  • Right: 90° (perfect corner)
  • Obtuse: 90° - 180° (large, wide)
  • Straight: 180° (flat line)
  • Reflex: 180° - 360° (long way around)

Important Relationships

Complementary Angles: Add to 90° → Comp = 90° - angle
Supplementary Angles: Add to 180° → Supp = 180° - angle
Vertically Opposite Angles: Always EQUAL
Angles on a Line: Add to 180°
Angles Around a Point: Add to 360°

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Complementary Angles
Find the complementary angle of 37°
Complementary = 90° - 37° = 53°
Answer: 53°
Example 2: Vertically Opposite Angles
Two lines cross. One angle is 68°. Find all four angles.
Angle A = 68°
Angle C (opposite) = 68°
Angle B (on line) = 180° - 68° = 112°
Angle D (opposite B) = 112°
Answer: 68°, 112°, 68°, 112°
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Supplementary Angles
If one angle is 157°, find its supplementary angle.
Answer: 180° - 157° = 23°
2. Angles Around a Point
Five angles around a point are 70°, 85°, 75°, 90°, and ?
Answer: 360° - (70+85+75+90) = 360° - 320° = 40°
TOPIC 2

Similar & Congruent Triangles

What You Need to Know

Congruent: Exactly the same (same size & shape)
Similar: Same shape, different size (proportional)

🎨 Curiosity: Map makers use similar triangles! When creating maps, they scale down real-world distances using similar triangles. A 100 km real distance might become 10 cm on a map, maintaining the same proportions!

Congruence Rules (3 ways to prove)

SSS: All 3 sides equal
SAS: Two sides & angle between equal
ASA: Two angles & side between equal

Similarity Rules (3 ways to prove)

AAA: All 3 angles equal
SSS Proportional: All sides in same ratio
SAS Proportional: Two sides in same ratio & angle equal

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Check if Congruent
Triangle A: sides 5, 7, 8. Triangle B: sides 5, 7, 8
All three sides match (SSS rule)
Answer: YES, CONGRUENT
Example 2: Check if Similar & Find Scale
Triangle A: sides 3, 4, 6. Triangle B: sides 6, 8, 12
Ratio: 3:6 = 1:2, 4:8 = 1:2, 6:12 = 1:2
All ratios are the same
Answer: YES, SIMILAR with scale 1:2
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Missing Side in Similar Triangle
Triangle A: sides 2, 3, 4. Triangle B: sides 4, 6, ?
Find the missing side if B is similar to A with ratio 1:2
Answer: 4 × 2 = 8
2. Real World Problem
A model car is 8 cm. Real car is 400 cm. Model door is 1.5 cm wide. How wide is real door?
Answer: Ratio = 400/8 = 50. Real door = 1.5 × 50 = 75 cm
TOPIC 3

Polygons

What You Need to Know

A polygon is a closed shape with straight sides. The name tells you how many sides!

🐝 Curiosity: Honeycomb uses HEXAGONS! Bees create hexagonal cells because hexagons use the least amount of wax while storing the most honey. It's nature's perfect geometry optimization!

Types of Polygons

Name Sides Sum of Angles
Triangle 3 180°
Quadrilateral 4 360°
Pentagon 5 540°
Hexagon 6 720°
Heptagon 7 900°
Octagon 8 1080°

Key Formula

Sum of Interior Angles = (n - 2) × 180°
where n = number of sides

Regular Polygons

In a regular polygon, all sides are equal and all angles are equal.

Each angle in regular polygon = [(n - 2) × 180°] ÷ n

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Sum of Angles in Pentagon
(5 - 2) × 180° = 3 × 180° = 540°
Answer: 540°
Example 2: Find Missing Angle
Hexagon with angles 100°, 110°, 120°, 130°, 125°, and ?
Sum = (6-2) × 180° = 720°
100+110+120+130+125 = 585°
Missing = 720° - 585° = 135°
Answer: 135°
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Each angle in regular octagon
Answer: [(8-2)×180°] ÷ 8 = 1080° ÷ 8 = 135°
2. Pentagon with angles 105°, 115°, 120°, 110°, and ?
Answer: 540° - 450° = 90°
TOPIC 4

Plane Shapes - Properties & Perimeter

What is Perimeter?

Perimeter is the distance AROUND the edge of a shape, measured in linear units (cm, m, etc.)

🏃 Curiosity: Running track perimeters are 400 meters (internationally standard)! The outer edge is 400m, but staggered start lines account for the different arc distances each runner runs.

Perimeter Formulas

Rectangle: P = 2(l + w)
Square: P = 4s
Parallelogram: P = 2(a + b)
Triangle: P = a + b + c
Trapezoid: P = a + b + c + d
Circle (Circumference): C = 2πr = πd (where π ≈ 3.14)

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Rectangle Perimeter
Length 15 cm, Width 8 cm
P = 2(15 + 8) = 2(23) = 46 cm
Answer: 46 cm
Example 2: Circle Circumference
Radius 7 cm (π ≈ 3.14)
C = 2 × 3.14 × 7 = 43.96 cm
Answer: 43.96 cm
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Parallelogram with sides 12 cm and 9 cm
Answer: P = 2(12 + 9) = 2(21) = 42 cm
2. Triangle with sides 5 cm, 6 cm, 7 cm
Answer: P = 5 + 6 + 7 = 18 cm
TOPIC 5

Area of Irregular Shapes

What You Need to Know

Irregular shapes don't have simple formulas. Break them into regular shapes or use special methods!

📍 Curiosity: Surveyors use the "breaking into triangles" method to measure irregular land plots! They triangulate from known points to calculate the exact area of property with irregular boundaries.

Three Methods

Method 1: Break into regular shapes → Add areas
Method 2: Subtract from larger shape → Large - Cut-outs
Method 3: Grid counting → Count full + partial squares

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: L-Shape (Method 1)
Vertical: 5×15, Horizontal: 8×6
Area 1 = 5 × 15 = 75 cm²
Area 2 = 8 × 6 = 48 cm²
Total = 75 + 48 = 123 cm²
Answer: 123 cm²
Example 2: Rectangle with Cut-Out (Method 2)
Large: 20×15, Cut-out: 3×4
Large area = 20 × 15 = 300 cm²
Cut-out = 3 × 4 = 12 cm²
Final = 300 - 12 = 288 cm²
Answer: 288 cm²
Practice Problems & Answers
1. T-Shape: Top 10×5, Bottom+sides 15×10 minus top overlaps
Answer: (15×10) - (2×2.5×5) = 150 - 25 = 125 cm² (or use method 1)
TOPIC 6

Area of Plane Shapes

What You Need to Know

Area is the space INSIDE a shape, measured in square units (cm², m², etc.)

🌾 Curiosity: Ancient Egyptians used area formulas to calculate tax on farmland! The more area your land, the more tax you paid. This is one reason geometry was so important in ancient Egypt!

All Area Formulas

Shape Formula
Rectangle A = l × w
Square A = s²
Triangle A = ½ × b × h
Parallelogram A = b × h
Trapezoid A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h
Circle A = πr²

Important Reminder

HEIGHT vs SIDE:
  • For triangles & parallelograms: Use PERPENDICULAR height, not slant side!
  • Height is the vertical distance, measured at 90° to the base

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Triangle Area
Base 14 cm, Height 9 cm
A = ½ × 14 × 9 = ½ × 126 = 63 cm²
Answer: 63 cm²
Example 2: Trapezoid Area
Bases 8 cm and 12 cm, Height 6 cm
A = ½ × (8 + 12) × 6 = ½ × 20 × 6 = ½ × 120 = 60 cm²
Answer: 60 cm²
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Circle with radius 8 cm (π ≈ 3.14)
Answer: A = 3.14 × 8² = 3.14 × 64 = 200.96 cm²
2. Parallelogram base 18 cm, height 7 cm
Answer: A = 18 × 7 = 126 cm²
TOPIC 7

Solid Shapes 1 - Cuboid, Cube & Cylinder

What You Need to Know

3D shapes have volume (space inside) and surface area (total exterior). Use CUBIC units for volume!

📦 Curiosity: Shipping boxes are often cuboids because they pack efficiently and stack easily! A cube is a special cuboid that uses the least wrapping paper for the most volume.

Formulas

Shape Volume Surface Area
Cuboid V = l × w × h SA = 2(lw + wh + lh)
Cube V = s³ SA = 6s²
Cylinder V = πr²h SA = 2πr² + 2πrh

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Cuboid Volume & SA
l=10, w=6, h=5
V = 10 × 6 × 5 = 300 cm³
SA = 2(60 + 30 + 50) = 2(140) = 280 cm²
Answer: V = 300 cm³, SA = 280 cm²
Example 2: Cylinder Volume
r=4, h=10 (π ≈ 3.14)
V = 3.14 × 4² × 10 = 3.14 × 16 × 10 = 502.4 cm³
Answer: 502.4 cm³
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Cube with side 6 cm
Answer: V = 6³ = 216 cm³, SA = 6×36 = 216 cm²
2. Cylinder r=3, h=8 (π≈3.14)
Answer: V = 3.14×9×8 = 226.08 cm³
TOPIC 8

Solid Shapes 2 - Cone, Pyramid & Frustum

What You Need to Know

These are tapered shapes (cone & pyramid) or truncated versions (frustum).

🗿 Curiosity: The Great Pyramids of Egypt are SQUARE PYRAMIDS! They were built so precisely that corners point to cardinal directions (North, South, East, West). Engineers used angle calculations similar to what you're learning!

Formulas

Shape Volume Formula
Cone V = ⅓πr²h
Pyramid (Square) V = ⅓ × s² × h
Frustum (Cone) V = ⅓πh(r₁² + r₂² + r₁r₂)

Key Facts

All use ⅓ (one-third)!
  • Cone volume = ⅓ of cylinder volume (same base & height)
  • Pyramid volume = ⅓ of cuboid volume (same base & height)
  • Frustum = cone/pyramid with top cut off
  • For frustum: r₁ = top radius, r₂ = bottom radius

Slant Height Formula

For cones: l² = h² + r²
(Pythagorean theorem - always find square root!)

Worked Examples with Solutions

Example 1: Cone Volume
r=5, h=12 (π ≈ 3.14)
V = ⅓ × 3.14 × 5² × 12
= ⅓ × 3.14 × 25 × 12 = ⅓ × 942 = 314 cm³
Answer: 314 cm³
Example 2: Pyramid Volume
Square base side 8 cm, height 10 cm
Base area = 8² = 64 cm²
V = ⅓ × 64 × 10 = ⅓ × 640 = 213.33 cm³
Answer: 213.33 cm³
Example 3: Frustum Volume
r₁=3, r₂=6, h=9 (π ≈ 3.14)
V = ⅓ × 3.14 × 9 × (9 + 36 + 18)
= ⅓ × 3.14 × 9 × 63 = ⅓ × 1,782.18 = 594.06 cm³
Answer: 594.06 cm³
Practice Problems & Answers
1. Cone with radius 7 cm, height 15 cm (π≈3.14)
Answer: V = ⅓×3.14×49×15 = ⅓×2,307.9 = 769.3 cm³
2. Slant height of cone with h=8, r=6
Answer: l² = 64+36 = 100, so l = √100 = 10 cm
FINAL TIPS

Exam Tips & Quick Reference

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Forgetting to take square root: If l² = 169, answer is l = 13, NOT l² = 169!
  • ❌ Mixing up formulas: Circle AREA = πr² (not 2πr!)
  • ❌ Using side instead of height: For triangles, ALWAYS use perpendicular height!
  • ❌ Forgetting ÷ 3: Cones and pyramids use ⅓ in volume!
  • ❌ Wrong units: Volume = cm³, Area = cm², Perimeter = cm

Remember These Key Values

π ≈ 3.14 (always!)
Full circle = 360°
Straight line = 180°
Right angle = 90°
√4 = 2, √9 = 3, √16 = 4, √25 = 5, √100 = 10, √169 = 13
